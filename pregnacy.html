How is the Due Date Calculated?

The most common method is Naegele's Rule — add 280 days (40 weeks) to the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP). This assumes a standard 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14. Our calculator automatically adjusts for your cycle length.

If you know your conception date, the due date is calculated by adding 266 days (38 weeks) from conception, since fertilization typically occurs about 14 days after your LMP.

Only about 5% of babies are born on their exact due date. Most births occur within two weeks before or after the estimated due date (EDD).

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is a due date calculator?
A due date calculator gives an estimate based on your last period or conception date. It's accurate to within 1–2 weeks in most cases. An ultrasound in the first trimester provides the most accurate dating. Only about 5% of babies arrive on their exact due date.
What is a full-term pregnancy?
A full-term pregnancy is 39–40 weeks. Early term is 37–38 weeks. Late term is 41 weeks. Post-term is 42 weeks or more. Babies born before 37 weeks are considered premature.
What are the three trimesters?
The first trimester covers weeks 1–13, the second trimester weeks 14–27, and the third trimester weeks 28–40. Each trimester brings different developmental milestones for your baby.
My cycle is not 28 days — does that affect my due date?
Yes. If your cycle is longer than 28 days, ovulation happens later, so your due date is later. If your cycle is shorter, your due date is earlier. Our calculator accounts for your cycle length automatically.
